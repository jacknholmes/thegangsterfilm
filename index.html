The Gangster Film

By Jack Noveck-Holmes

Gangster films have been a staple of cinema since the early 20th century, captivating audiences with tales of organized crime, complex characters and moral ambiguity.

The making of The Godfather, a crowning achievement of the genre, was deeply influenced by the real-life Mafia, albeit in an indirect manner. The production faced numerous challenges from Joe Colombo, the founder of the Italian-American Civil Rights League, and his associates, who believed that the film would boost the stereotype of Italian-Americans being involved in organized crime. As it turned out, Colombo was the boss of a New York City crime family himself. This opposition led to negotiations and compromises between producers and members of the Colombo Crime Family. Ultimately, Colombo approved of the film when the word 'Mafia' was cut out from its script by producers. Above is a scene from the television series The Offer, which chronicles the making of The Godfather. In the scene, Joe Colombo and Al Ruddy, a producer of The Godfather, meet for the first time.
Father and son in The Godfather saga, Vito (left) and Michael Corleone (right) starkly contrast each other in their priorities while acting as boss of the Corleone family. Vito, the family's patriarch, operates with his family in mind. His actions and decisions, while morally ambiguous, are motivated by his need to support his family. Michael, on the other hand, is far more cold-blooded when he becomes the family's boss, with business, profit and power taking precedent. Michael's ruthlessness is on full display in the second film, in which he's fully matured into the position of boss.

From long tracking shots to the use of popular music, Goodfellas director Martin Scorsese has employed a number of techniques to his films that have become synonymous with his style. Of these characteristic features is a documentary style approach. No moment displays this feature better than the above scene from Goodfellas, in which protagonist Henry Hill uses narration and a first person point of view to introduce the audience to fellow neighborhood mobsters.
Scarface screenwriter Oliver Stone is known for crafting many of his films with a particular political angle in mind. His political fingerprints are all over Scarface, with the film's opening scene (above) showing documentary footage of the Mariel Boatlift, an event in 1980 in which Fidel Castro sent a sizable number of Cuban criminals, individuals in mental institutions and other undesired civilians on a series of boats to South Florida. It is on this series of boats that the film's main character, Tony Montana, emigrated to Miami. The scene is the first of many examples of the historical context that creates the backdrop for Montana's story.
